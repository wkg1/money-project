## Thread 2: Why Most Productivity Tools Fail ADHD Brains (And What Actually Works)

**Tweet 1/8 (HOOK)**
I have ADHD. I have tried every productivity system that exists.

Bullet journaling. GTD. Pomodoro. Todoist. TickTick. 6 different Notion templates.

They all failed within 2 weeks.

Here is why — and what I finally built that has worked for 4 months straight:

**Tweet 2/8**
The failure pattern was always identical:

Day 1-3: Hyperfocus during setup. Excited. This is the one.
Day 4-6: Novelty fading. Starting to skip steps.
Day 7-14: Complete abandonment. Back to chaos.

It was not a discipline problem. It was a design problem.

**Tweet 3/8**
Neurotypical productivity tools violate 3 things ADHD brains need:

1. Friction-free capture (if it takes more than 2 clicks, it will not happen)
2. Immediate visual feedback (we need to SEE progress or it does not exist)
3. Externalized memory (our working memory is unreliable — capture now or lose it forever)

**Tweet 4/8**
So I designed a system backwards from how my brain actually works.

Three components. That is it.

1. BRAIN DUMP ZONE — 3 seconds to capture any thought. Auto-sorted. No categories, no friction.

2. TODAY'S FOCUS — 3 tasks max. Not 50. Each matched to energy level. Finish 3, the day is a win.

3. DONE LIST — track what you completed, not what you failed to do. Weekly visual wall of wins.

**Tweet 5/8**
The design rules I followed:

- Maximum 2 steps for any action
- Visual progress that updates immediately
- Missing a day has zero penalty (guilt kills ADHD motivation)
- No "you fell behind" shame messages
- Every completion gives a small reward hit

**Tweet 6/8**
Results after 4 months:

- First system I have used consistently since diagnosis
- Task completion rate up ~60% (I actually finish things now)
- Brain dump zone has 400+ captured thoughts that would have been lost
- Weekly review shows visible progress instead of a blank void of "what did I even do"

**Tweet 7/8**
The wildest part: the system works BECAUSE it does less, not more.

Every other tool tried to add features. More databases. More integrations. More views.

ADHD brains do not need more. They need less friction and more reward.

## Thread 3: I Analyzed 50 Top Developer Portfolios. Here Is What the Best Ones Have in Common.

**Tweet 1/7 (HOOK)**
I spent last weekend analyzing 50 top-performing developer portfolios (the ones that actually get hired).

Here are the 6 patterns every standout portfolio follows — and the template I built from them:

**Tweet 2/7**
PATTERN 1: Bento Grid Layout

Every top portfolio I looked at uses modular grid cards. Not a long scrolling page of text. Not a carousel. Bento grids (like Apple's design system) show everything at once with natural visual hierarchy.

Big cards = important. Small cards = supporting info.

**Tweet 3/7**
PATTERN 2: Dark Theme with Accent Colors

48 out of 50 portfolios used dark backgrounds. The best ones used a single accent color (purple, cyan, or gradient) for CTAs and highlights. Light mode portfolios looked dated.

Dark bg + 3-4 accent colors + white text = 2026 standard.

**Tweet 4/7**
PATTERN 3: Hero Section With Stats, Not Paragraphs

The worst portfolios: "I am a passionate developer with 5 years of experience..."

The best portfolios: Name, title, 3-4 stats (projects, clients, years, awards), one-line value prop. That is it.

Numbers > adjectives.

**Tweet 5/7**
PATTERN 4: Featured Project Gets 2x Space

Every top portfolio had one "hero" project that was visually dominant — spanning 2x2 grid cells with a project tag, description, and skill pills.

The other projects got single-cell cards. The hierarchy tells the viewer where to look first.

**Tweet 6/7**
PATTERNS 5 & 6:

5. Contact section is a separate card with a clear CTA button (not a footer link). "Let's Work Together" or "Say Hello" with a prominent button.

6. Social links are present but minimal — GitHub, LinkedIn, Twitter. No icon explosions. Just text links in muted colors.
