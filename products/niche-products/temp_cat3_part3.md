### SOCIAL PROMPT 9: Client Success Story

**When To Use:** Sharing a past client's success story on social media. These posts humanize you, provide social proof, and are among the highest-engagement content types for real estate agents.

**Context To Gather:**
- Client first names (get permission)
- What their situation was (buying, selling, both)
- A specific challenge or obstacle they faced
- How you helped solve it
- The happy outcome (closed, moved in, etc.)
- A quote from the client (get permission)
- A photo of the client at closing, in front of the home, or similar

**The Prompt:**

```
You are a content writer for a real estate agent creating a client success story post. This is not a testimonial. It is a story where the client is the hero and you played a supporting role. The post should inspire, not brag.

Write a client success story social media post. Follow these rules:
- Open with the client's situation and challenge (make them relatable)
- Include the moment of tension or difficulty (what almost went wrong)
- Describe how you helped in specific, concrete terms (not "I guided them," but "I called the listing agent at 9 PM and negotiated...")
- End with the happy outcome and a client quote if available
- Include a photo description
- Keep it about the client, not you. You are the supporting character.
- Length: 120-180 words
- Tone: Heartwarming, genuine, understated. Proud of the client, not yourself.

STORY DETAILS:
- Client Name(s): [INSERT first names]
- Situation: [INSERT were they buying, selling, etc.]
- Challenge: [INSERT what was hard]
- How You Helped: [INSERT specific actions]
- Outcome: [INSERT result]
- Client Quote: [INSERT if available]
- Photo: [INSERT description]
```

**Example Output:**

> *For first-time buyers who won a bidding war:*
>
> Jordan and Priya started looking for their first home in January. By March, they had lost out on three offers -- two to all-cash buyers and one to someone who waived inspection entirely. They were doing everything right and still losing, and it was wearing on them.
>
> When the fourth house came on the market -- a 3-bedroom bungalow in their preferred neighborhood -- we changed the strategy. Instead of competing on price (they could not outbid cash), we competed on terms. We wrote an offer with a 21-day close (their lender could do it), an as-is inspection rider with a pass/fail clause (they could walk but not renegotiate), and a personal letter from Jordan and Priya that the listing agent later told me was the reason the sellers chose their offer over a higher one.

**Example Output:**

> Staging is not decorating. It is editing. You are not showing buyers how YOU live. You are showing them how THEY could live. Those are two very different houses.
>
> Five things that matter more than you think:
>
> **1. Remove 50% of your stuff. Then remove 30% more.** (Countertops, shelves, closets. Buyers open everything. Half-empty closets look spacious. Full closets look inadequate. This one is free and it is the single highest-ROI thing you can do.)
>
> **2. Every room needs ONE clear purpose.** That bedroom you turned into a storage/craft/guest room hybrid? Pick one. Confusion kills offers.
>

>

> **5. The front door is the first handshake.** Power wash the porch. New doormat. Fresh flowers or a green plant by the door. Paint the front door if it is even slightly chipped. >
> I had a seller last year who spent $1,200 on staging (decluttering help + a few rental pieces for the living room). Her home sold for $15,000 over asking in four days. The identical floor plan down the street, unstaged, sat for 42 days and closed at $8,000 under.
>
> Staging is not an expense. It is part of your marketing budget. And the data says it returns 5-10x what you spend.

```
Write a mortgage education social media post. Follow these rules:

- Give one specific, actionable piece of advice
- Include a relevant example with real numbers
- End with a disclaimer (I am not a lender / consult a professional)
- Length: 150-200 words
- Tone: Educational, reassuring, clear. You are translating a confusing system into simple choices.

MORTGAGE TOPIC DETAILS:
- Topic: [INSERT]
- Common Misconception: [INSERT]
- Actionable Tip: [INSERT]
- Example Numbers: [INSERT]
- Current Rate Context: [INSERT]
```

**Example Output:**

> *For a post about rate buydowns:*
>
> I have had three buyers this month ask me: "Should I wait for rates to drop before I buy?"
>
> Here is what they did not know: you do not have to wait for rates to drop. You can buy the rate down now, and in many cases, the seller can pay for it.
>
> A 2-1 temporary buydown works like this: Let us say the current rate is 6.5%. With a 2-1 buydown, your rate is 4.5% in year one, 5.5% in year two, and 6.5% from year three onward. The cost is typically 2-3% of the loan amount, and in a buyer's market (or even a balanced one), you can negotiate for the seller to cover that cost as a closing concession.
>
> On a $400,000 loan, the buydown costs about $8,000-$10,000 and saves you roughly $500/month in year one and $250/month in year two. That is $9,000 in savings over two years.
>
> The bet you are making: rates will be lower in two years and you will refinance before the buydown expires. If rates stay high, you still saved $9,000.
>
> Ask your lender about seller-paid temporary buydowns. If they do not know what you are talking about, get a different lender.
>
> (Reminder: I am a real estate agent, not a mortgage lender. This is educational, not financial advice. Consult your lender for your specific situation.)

> *For a "Two Weeks Before Moving" checklist:*
>
> Two weeks out. This is the phase where "we have plenty of time" turns into "why do we own so much stuff" very quickly. Here is what actually matters:
>
> [ ] **Confirm your movers.** Call them. Do not text, do not email. Call and confirm the date, time, and truck size. Verify they have your new address. Movers get double-booked more often than you think.
>
> [ ] **Start packing the "open first" box.** This is the box that rides in your car, not the truck. Contents: toilet paper, paper towels, hand soap, trash bags, phone chargers, a shower curtain, one set of sheets per bed, a change of clothes, basic tools (screwdriver, hammer, box cutter), paper plates/plastic utensils, and a bottle of wine (or whatever your moving-day reward looks like).
