### COMMUNICATION PROMPT 6: Home Anniversary Check-In

**When To Use:** On the 1-year anniversary of a client's home purchase. This is a relationship-maintenance touch that often generates referrals and repeat business.

**Context To Gather:**
- Client name(s) and property address
- Closing date
- Current estimated home value (from a quick CMA)
- Equity estimate (if meaningful)
- A genuine question about how they are enjoying the home
- A market update relevant to their situation

**The Prompt:**

```
You are a real estate agent writing a 1-year home anniversary check-in to past clients. This is not a sales email disguised as a check-in. It is a genuine touchpoint that happens to include useful information and a soft door opening for future business.

Write a home anniversary check-in. Follow these rules:
- Lead with the anniversary acknowledgment -- it makes them feel remembered
- Provide real, useful information: estimated current value and equity
- Ask a genuine question about their life/home
- Offer value: a market update, a service provider recommendation, or a home maintenance tip
- Mention referrals or future business as an afterthought, not the purpose
- Length: 150-200 words
- Tone: Warm, helpful, genuine. You actually care how they are doing.

ANNIVERSARY DETAILS:
- Client Name(s): [INSERT]
- Address: [INSERT]
- Closing Date: [INSERT]
- Estimated Current Value: [INSERT]
- Estimated Equity: [INSERT]
- Genuine Question: [INSERT something specific to them]
- Helpful Info To Share: [INSERT market update or tip]
```

**Example Output:**

> Subject: One year at 1427 Ashland. How is the porch treating you?
>
> Hey Sarah and Marcus,
>
> One year ago today, you picked up the keys to 1427 Ashland Avenue. I remember standing on that wraparound porch with you after closing when Sarah said, "I am going to drink coffee here every single morning." Did that hold up?
>
> A quick market update you might like: homes in Oak Park have appreciated about 6% over the past 12 months, and similar bungalows to yours have been selling in the $410,000-$425,000 range. Rough estimate -- you are sitting on about $35,000-$45,000 in equity after one year. Not bad for drinking coffee on a porch.
>
> Also, I know the baby is getting mobile (she has to be crawling by now, right?). If you have not already, this is the month to baby-proof -- cabinet locks, furniture anchors, outlet covers. I have a handyman who does baby-proofing installs and he is fast and reasonable. Text me if you want his number.
>
> How is the house treating you? Any surprises, good or bad? And if anyone you know is thinking about making a move, I would love to help them the way I hope I helped you.
>
> Hope you are loving the neighborhood. Go sit on that porch for me.
>
> [Your Name]

### COMMUNICATION PROMPT 7: Expired Listing Re-Approach

**When To Use:** When a listing expires with another agent and you want to position yourself as the solution. This is a sensitive communication because the seller may be frustrated, disappointed, or defensive.

**Context To Gather:**
- Property address and expired list price
- Days on market and any price reductions
- Why you believe it did not sell (price, marketing, condition, market timing)
- Your specific strategy for getting it sold (different from the previous agent)
- 2-3 specific marketing or pricing tactics you will use differently
- Your track record with similar properties

**The Prompt:**

```
You are a real estate agent writing to a homeowner whose listing just expired. You need to be respectful of their situation (they are likely frustrated), honest about why the home didn't sell, and confident that you can get a different result.

Write an expired listing re-approach letter. Follow these rules:
- Do not criticize the previous agent. Focus on the market, the pricing strategy, or the marketing approach -- never the person
- Acknowledge the frustration without dwelling on it
- Present 2-3 specific, concrete things you will do differently (not generic "better marketing")
- Include a relevant success story if you have one (comparable property you sold after another agent couldn't)
- Make a specific invitation to meet or talk -- do not leave it open-ended
- Length: 250-350 words
- Tone: Respectful, analytical, confident. You have answers, not just enthusiasm.

EXPIRED LISTING DETAILS:
- Address: [INSERT]
- Expired Price: [INSERT]
- DOM: [INSERT]
- Price Reductions: [INSERT]
- Why It Didn't Sell (your analysis): [INSERT]
- Your Different Strategy: [INSERT 2-3 tactics]
- Your Track Record: [INSERT relevant success story]
- Meeting Invitation: [INSERT specific ask]
```

**Example Output:**

> Subject: 312 Brookstone Drive -- A different approach
>
> Dear Mr. and Mrs. Henderson,
>
> I noticed your listing at 312 Brookstone Drive expired last week after 94 days on the market. I want to be direct with you about what I think happened, and what I would do differently.
>
> I walked through the home at the broker's open in March. The kitchen is outstanding. The lot is one of the best in Brookstone -- private, flat, and it backs to the greenway. This is not a house that failed to sell because of the house. It failed to sell for two reasons I can identify, and both are fixable.
>
> First, the price. At $635,000, you were competing with new construction in the same school district. Buyers at that price point could get a brand-new home with a builder warranty, and several of them did. The comps that actually closed in Brookstone over the past six months all fell in the $565,000-$590,000 range. That's where the buyers are. The previous agent should have shown you those numbers.
>
> Second, the marketing did not reach the right buyer. The listing photos were fine, but there was no video walkthrough, no drone photography, and no targeted digital advertising. Brookstone is a neighborhood that attracts buyers relocating from out of state -- those buyers are searching YouTube walks and Instagram, not just Zillow. If your buyer lives in Chicago or Dallas right now, they never saw your home.
>
> Here is what I would do:
>
> 1. Reposition at $585,000, supported by a fresh CMA that I will walk through with you in detail. At this price, your home is the clear best value in Brookstone, and we create a competitive situation that drives the price up rather than chasing the market down.
>
> 2. Full visual marketing package: architectural photography (not real estate photography -- there is a difference), drone video, interior walkthrough, and a targeted ad campaign to buyers searching for Brookstone-area homes from outside the market. I spend $800/month on ads and I cover it.
>
> 3. I sold 2411 Brookstone (four streets over, similar square footage, same floor plan) last November in 11 days with three offers. It had been listed for 60 days with another brokerage and expired before I took it over. The seller got 98% of asking. I would be happy to share those details.
>
> I would love to spend 30 minutes at your kitchen table going through the numbers and showing you exactly what a relaunch looks like. If you are open to it, what morning this week works?
>
> Sincerely,
> [Your Name]

### COMMUNICATION PROMPT 8: FSBO (For Sale By Owner) Pitch

**When To Use:** Approaching a homeowner who is attempting to sell without an agent. This communication must demonstrate value immediately, as FSBO sellers are skeptical of agent commissions.

**Context To Gather:**
- Property address and FSBO asking price
- How long they have been on the market
- Recent comparable sales (to build a realistic price discussion)
- Data on FSBO vs. agent-represented sale prices (the NAR statistic: FSBO homes sell for approximately 26% less)
- Your specific value proposition (what you do that justifies your commission)
- An offer or concession (reduced commission, limited-service option, etc.)

**The Prompt:**

```
You are a real estate agent writing to a For Sale By Owner seller. You know they are skeptical of agents and commissions. You need to demonstrate value in the first three sentences, back it up with data, and make a no-pressure offer that costs them nothing to hear.

Write a FSBO pitch communication. Follow these rules:
- Acknowledge their effort and resourcefulness -- do not condescend
- Use data to make your case (average FSBO sale price vs. agent-represented, days on market, etc.)
- Address the commission concern directly: show them how net proceeds can be HIGHER with an agent even after commission
- Offer 2-3 specific things you do that they cannot do themselves (MLS access, negotiation, buyer vetting, etc.)
- Make a low-commitment offer: a conversation, not a listing presentation
- Length: 200-300 words
- Tone: Respectful, data-driven, confident. You are a business case, not an interruption.

FSBO DETAILS:
- Address: [INSERT]
- Asking Price: [INSERT]
- Days on Market: [INSERT if known]
- Relevant Data Point: [INSERT NAR stat or local data]
- Your Value Props: [INSERT 2-3]
- Low-Commitment Offer: [INSERT meeting or call invite]
```

**Example Output:**

> Subject: 318 Cedar Lane -- I can help you net more (even after my commission)
>
> Hi Mr. Tillman,
>
> I drive past your FSBO sign on Cedar Lane every morning. Respect for putting in the work -- selling on your own takes guts and effort and most people do not have either.
>
> I also notice the sign has been up for about three weeks and the home is still showing as available. That tracks with what the data says: the typical FSBO home takes more than twice as long to sell as an agent-represented home.
>
> Here is the number I think you should know: according to the National Association of Realtors, FSBO homes sold for a median of $310,000 in the last survey year, while agent-assisted homes sold for a median of $405,000. Same market, same buyers, massively different result. And that gap -- even after a 5-6% commission -- puts more money in the seller's pocket.
>
> Why the gap? Three things you cannot easily replicate on your own:
>
> 1. MLS and syndication: your home shows up on Zillow, but it is not in the MLS, which means buyer's agents do not get paid to show it, which means they avoid it. I can change that and still negotiate a commission structure that works for both of us.
>
> 2. Buyer vetting: tire-kickers and "just curious" neighbors waste your weekends. I screen for pre-approvals before anyone walks through the door.
>
> 3. Negotiation: when a buyer makes an offer, you are negotiating against someone who likely has an agent. The commission you save upfront often gets taken back in the negotiation by a buyer who knows you are unrepresented.
>
> I am not asking for a listing agreement. I am asking for a 20-minute conversation where I show you the net-sheet math and you decide if the numbers make sense. If they do not, no hard feelings and I will keep driving past your sign wishing you the best.
>
> Would Wednesday evening or Saturday morning work for a quick chat?

### COMMUNICATION PROMPT 9: Past Client Referral Request

**When To Use:** Periodic outreach to your past client database to generate referrals. This should be done 2-4 times per year and must feel like a natural check-in, not a marketing blast.

**Context To Gather:**
- Past client name(s) and transaction type (buyer/seller)
- How long since their transaction
- A market update relevant to their specific situation
- A specific favor or ask related to referrals
- An incentive (if you offer one -- gift card, charity donation, etc.)
- A genuine reason you are reaching out now

**The Prompt:**

```
You are a real estate agent writing to a past client to ask for referrals. This email must feel personal, not mass-sent. It should provide value first, ask second.

Write a referral request email. Follow these rules:
- Open with something personal or a relevant market observation
- Provide genuine value first (a tip, a market stat, a recommendation)
- Make the ask specific: "Do you know ONE person..." rather than "send me referrals"
- Explain why referrals matter to your business (makes it personal, not corporate)
- Offer something in return (a gift, a donation, a favor)
- Make it easy to say "no" -- no guilt
- Length: 150-200 words
- Tone: Warm, personal, grateful. You are asking a friend for a favor.

REFERRAL DETAILS:
- Client Name(s): [INSERT]
- Transaction: [INSERT bought/sold, when]
- Value To Share: [INSERT market tip or observation]
- Specific Ask: [INSERT "one person" framing]
- Incentive: [INSERT if any]
- Reason For Timing: [INSERT why now]
```

**Example Output:**

> Subject: Quick favor (and a market stat you might like)
>
> Hi Rachel,
>
> Hope the family is good and the garden you planted in April survived the summer. (Mine absolutely did not. Zero for three on tomato plants this year.)
>
> Quick market stat I thought you would find interesting: homes in your neighborhood are up 7% since you bought two years ago. Based on the comps I pulled this week, your place is likely worth $40K-$50K more than you paid. Just thought you would like to know.
>
> I am going to ask a favor, and if the answer is no, that is genuinely fine.
>
> Do you know ONE person right now who is thinking about buying or selling? A coworker, a neighbor, a friend from the gym?
>
> My business runs almost entirely on referrals from people like you. It means I can spend less on ads and more on actually serving my clients. When you send someone my way, they get the same buyer's agent experience you got -- and I make sure of it.
>
> If someone comes to mind, a quick intro email or text is all it takes. And if not, no worries at all. I will check back in when I have another good market stat to share.
>
> Thanks for even reading this far.
>
> [Your Name]
