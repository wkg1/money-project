### COMMUNICATION PROMPT 10: Market Update Newsletter

**When To Use:** A monthly or quarterly email newsletter to your database covering local real estate market conditions. This positions you as a market authority and keeps you top-of-mind.

**Context To Gather:**
- Current average/median home price (year-over-year change)
- Average days on market
- Inventory levels (months of supply)
- Interest rate environment
- 1-2 notable recent sales or listings
- A practical tip (buying, selling, or home ownership)
- Any regulatory or market changes affecting buyers/sellers

**The Prompt:**

```
You are a real estate agent writing a monthly market update newsletter for your database. This is not a sales pitch. It is a value-add communication that positions you as the person who understands the market better than anyone else.

Write a market update newsletter. Follow these rules:
- Lead with the single most important market story this month -- the one sentence that matters
- Present statistics in context, not as raw numbers (e.g., "up 8% from last year" not "median price is $385,000")
- Translate market conditions into buyer/seller implications: "this means..."
- Include one "what to do about it" piece of practical advice
- Add a brief personal note or local color that makes it feel like it was written by a human
- Do not make this about you or your listings
- Length: 300-400 words
- Tone: Informative, balanced, human. A smart friend explaining what's happening.

MARKET DATA:
- Month/Year: [INSERT]
- Median Price & YOY Change: [INSERT]
- Avg DOM: [INSERT]
- Inventory/Months Supply: [INSERT]
- Interest Rates: [INSERT]
- Notable Sales: [INSERT 1-2]
- Practical Tip: [INSERT]
- Regulatory/Market Changes: [INSERT]
```

**Example Output:**

> Subject: May Market Update -- The spring market is doing something unusual this year
>
> Hi everyone,
>
> The big story in our market right now: inventory is climbing, but prices are not softening. That is unusual. Normally, more homes for sale means prices ease up a bit. Not this spring.
>
> Here are the numbers for the metro area, April 2026:
>
> Median sale price hit $412,000, which is up 8% from April 2025 and 3% from last month. Homes are still selling fast -- average days on market is 18 days, which is actually three days faster than last April even though there are more homes to choose from.
>
> Active inventory is up 14% year-over-year. That sounds like a lot, and it is a welcome change from the 2023-2024 drought, but we are still at 2.1 months of supply. For reference: 6 months is a balanced market. We are still firmly in seller territory, just a slightly less frantic version of it.
>
> What this means if you are buying: you have more options than you did last year, and you are slightly less likely to face 15 competing offers. You still need to be pre-approved, decisive, and ready to go the moment you find the right house. The buyers who are still "just looking" are losing homes to the buyers who have done their homework.
>
> What this means if you are selling: the market is still very much in your favor, but the days of listing at any price and getting multiple offers are behind us. Pricing accurately on day one matters more than ever, because buyers have options now and they will skip overpriced listings rather than bid them down.
>
> Interest rates: hovering right around 6.25% for a 30-year conventional. Not exciting, but not terrible, and they have been flat for three months. Stability is its own kind of good news.
>
> One practical thing: if you are thinking about selling in the next 12 months, May and June are still historically the best months to list. Fall is fine. Winter is slower but the buyers who are out in winter are serious. There is no bad time to sell in this market -- just slightly better times.
>
> On a personal note: the farmers market at Riverside Park opened last weekend and the strawberries are already incredible. Go early before the heat sets in. Summer is basically here.
>
> If you have questions about your specific situation -- what your home might be worth, what you can afford, whether now is the right time to make a move -- reply to this email or text me. I am happy to run the numbers for you, no obligation.
>
> Talk soon,
> [Your Name]

### COMMUNICATION PROMPT 11: First-Time Buyer Education Series

**When To Use:** A nurturing email sequence for first-time buyers who are 3-12 months from purchasing. The goal is education and trust-building, not immediate conversion.

**Context To Gather:**
- Topic for this specific email in the series (pre-approval, neighborhoods, inspections, closing costs, etc.)
- The one key thing they need to understand about this topic
- A common mistake first-time buyers make
- Your personal insight or story related to this topic
- Next step in the educational journey

**The Prompt:**

```
You are a real estate agent writing one email in a series educating first-time homebuyers. This email should make them feel smarter and more prepared after reading it. You are not selling your services -- you are selling the idea that buying a home is achievable and that being informed protects them.

Write a first-time buyer education email. Follow these rules:
- Open with a relatable question or scenario that makes the reader feel seen
- Teach ONE specific thing thoroughly (do not try to cover everything)
- Include a "most people don't know this" insight
- Warn against a common mistake gently and constructively
- End with a small action they can take this week
- Length: 250-350 words
- Tone: Patient, educational, encouraging. Like a teacher who actually wants their students to succeed.

EMAIL DETAILS:
- Topic: [INSERT specific topic]
- Key Lesson: [INSERT one thing to learn]
- Common Mistake: [INSERT pitfall to avoid]
- Personal Insight: [INSERT your experience]
- Weekly Action: [INSERT small step]
```

**Example Output:**

> Subject: The mortgage pre-approval mistake that costs people the house
>
> Hi Taylor,
>
> Quick question: when you picture yourself buying a home, do you picture finding the house first and then figuring out the mortgage?
>
> If yes, you are in very good company. Most first-time buyers do it in that order. And most first-time buyers who do it in that order end up losing the first house they fall in love with. Here is why.
>
> A pre-qualification and a pre-approval are not the same thing. A pre-qualification is a 10-minute phone call where a lender asks about your income and pulls a credit report. It gives you a rough number. It is useful for knowing your ballpark budget. But when you submit an offer, the listing agent is going to look at a pre-qualification letter and think: "This buyer has not been vetted."
>
> A pre-approval is different. It means the lender has actually verified your income, assets, and employment. They have run your file through underwriting -- not all the way, but far enough to say, "Yes, this person can borrow this amount." A pre-approval letter is essentially the lender saying: barring an appraisal disaster, this loan closes.
>
> In a market where homes get multiple offers, the buyer with the pre-approval letter wins over the buyer with the pre-qualification letter, even if the offers are identical. I have seen it happen over and over.
>
> The common mistake: buyers get pre-qualified, think they are done, start touring homes, find the one, and then scramble to get pre-approved while competing buyers are already submitting offers. By the time their pre-approval comes through, the house is under contract.
>
> Here is what I want you to do this week, if you have not already: contact a lender (I can recommend a few who are really good with first-time buyers) and get fully pre-approved. It takes about a week, it costs nothing, and it turns you from a "maybe" buyer into a "ready" buyer overnight.
>
> Once you have that letter in hand, we can start looking at houses for real -- and when we find the right one, you will be able to move on it.
>
> Talk soon,
> [Your Name]

### COMMUNICATION PROMPT 12: Open House Follow-Up

**When To Use:** Following up with visitors who attended your open house. The goal is to qualify their interest level and convert serious buyers into clients.

**Context To Gather:**
- Open house date and property address
- Visitor name and any personal details you collected
- What they liked or disliked about the property
- Their current housing situation (renting, own, timeline)
- If they are working with another agent
- The property's best features that align with their interests
- Similar properties you could show them

**The Prompt:**

```
You are a real estate agent following up with someone who attended your open house. Your goal is to continue the conversation, determine if they are a serious buyer, and either pair them with this property or pivot to helping them find the right one.

Write an open house follow-up email. Follow these rules:
- Reference something specific they said or noticed at the open house (shows you remember them individually)
- Ask a qualifying question that helps you understand their seriousness
- If they liked this property, give them a reason to act soon (other interest, offers, etc.)
- If this property is not right for them, pivot immediately to finding what IS right
- Include one piece of information about this home or the market they probably did not get from just walking through
- Length: 150-200 words
- Tone: Casual, curious, helpful. You met them at a house, now you are following up like a normal person.

OPEN HOUSE DETAILS:
- Property Address: [INSERT]
- Open House Date: [INSERT]
- Visitor Name: [INSERT]
- What They Liked: [INSERT]
- What They Said: [INSERT specific comment]
- Their Situation: [INSERT renting/owning, timeline]
- Working With Agent: [INSERT yes/no/unknown]
- Similar Properties: [INSERT 1-2]
- Urgency Factor: [INSERT other interest in the property]
```

**Example Output:**

> Subject: That kitchen at 1427 Ashland though
>
> Hi Allison,
>
> It was great meeting you at the Ashland Avenue open house on Saturday. You mentioned the kitchen "ruined you for every other house you've seen," which I took as a compliment on behalf of the sellers.

> Either way, I would love to understand what you are looking for and whether it makes sense for me to help. Are you working with an agent already? Totally fine if so -- I do not poach. If not, I would be happy to set up a few showings, starting with that screened porch.
>
> Let me know what feels right.
>

### COMMUNICATION PROMPT 13: Showing Feedback Request

**When To Use:** Requesting feedback from a buyer's agent after they have shown your listing. This information helps your seller understand how the market is responding.

**Context To Gather:**
- Listing address
- Buyer agent name and brokerage
- Showing date and time
- Any preliminary feedback you already received
- Updates about the property (price changes, new offers, etc.)
- A specific question that yields useful feedback

**The Prompt:**

```
You are a listing agent requesting feedback from a buyer's agent after a showing. You need honest, actionable feedback that you can share with your seller, and you need it quickly.

Write a showing feedback request. Follow these rules:
- Make it incredibly short and easy to respond to (agents are busy and get dozens of these)
- Ask 2-3 specific questions, not "what did you think?"
- If you have a status update (offers received, price change, open house coming), include it -- it creates urgency
- Make responding easy: "reply with a number" or "one sentence is fine"
- Thank them genuinely for showing the property
- Length: Under 100 words (shorter is better)
- Tone: Efficient, appreciative, professional.

FEEDBACK REQUEST DETAILS:
- Listing Address: [INSERT]
- Buyer Agent Name: [INSERT]
- Showing Date/Time: [INSERT]
- Property Updates: [INSERT any]
- Specific Questions: [INSERT 2-3]
```

**Example Output:**

> Subject: Quick feedback on 1842 Ridgeway -- 2 questions
>
> Hi Mike,
>
> Thanks for showing 1842 Ridgeway to your clients. Quick favor -- two questions:
>
> 1. On a scale of 1-10, how interested were your buyers?

>
> One sentence each is perfect. This helps me give the sellers real feedback instead of guessing.

> Quick update: we have two showings today and an open house Sunday 1-4. No offers yet, but strong first-week traffic.

### COMMUNICATION PROMPT 14: Rate Change Communication

**When To Use:** When mortgage interest rates move significantly (up or down), providing timely perspective to buyers and sellers in your database.

**Context To Gather:**
- Current rate and the recent change (how much movement, over what period)
- What the rate change means for a typical buyer's monthly payment
- Historical rate context (we are not in unprecedented territory even if rates feel high)
- Actionable advice for buyers and sellers
- A mortgage professional quote or resource for deeper questions

**The Prompt:**

```
You are a real estate agent writing to your database about a recent interest rate change. Your goal is to inform, contextualize, and reduce anxiety. Rate changes create fear and hesitation -- your job is to bring clarity.

Write a rate change communication. Follow these rules:
- State the rate change clearly in the first sentence (what happened, by how much)
- Translate the change into real monthly payment dollars on a typical home in your market
- Provide historical context (are we high vs. last year? vs. last decade? vs. the 1980s?)
- Give specific, differentiated advice: what buyers should do, what sellers should consider
- Include the "marry the house, date the rate" perspective if rates went up
- If rates went down, help buyers understand what they can now afford
- Length: 200-300 words
- Tone: Calm, factual, helpful. You are the steady voice in a noisy market.

RATE DETAILS:
- Current Rate: [INSERT]
- Recent Change: [INSERT direction and magnitude]
- Monthly Payment Impact: [INSERT on a sample home price]
- Historical Context: [INSERT comparison points]
- Buyer Advice: [INSERT]
- Seller Advice: [INSERT]
- Lender Contact: [INSERT if applicable]
```

**Example Output:**

> Subject: Rates ticked up -- here is what it actually means for your monthly payment
>
> Hey everyone,
>
> Mortgage rates moved from 6.25% to 6.5% this week. I want to tell you what that actually means in dollars, because the headlines make it sound scarier than it is.
>
> On a $400,000 home with 20% down, the rate increase from 6.25% to 6.5% adds about $42 to your monthly payment. That is not nothing. It is also not a reason to stop looking.
>
> For some context: yes, rates are higher than the 3% we saw in 2021. Those rates were historic lows, not normal, and they are probably never coming back. But 6.5% is right around the 30-year historical average. My parents bought their first house in the mid-80s at 12%. We are not in unprecedented territory. We are in normal territory that feels painful because it followed an abnormal period.
>
> My advice if you are buying: do not try to time rates. If you find the right house at a price you can afford, buy it. When rates come down -- and they will eventually -- you refinance. The saying in our industry is "marry the house, date the rate." The house you do not buy because you are waiting for rates to drop is the house you will never get back.
>
> My advice if you are selling: rate increases shrink the buyer pool slightly, but serious buyers adjust. The last three homes I sold closed at or above asking even as rates ticked up, because inventory is still tight and life still happens -- people get married, have kids, change jobs, and need to move regardless of rates.
>
> If you want to run the numbers on your specific situation -- whether buying or selling -- reply to this email or text me. I have a lender I work with who can give you a real pre-approval and tell you exactly what your monthly payment looks like.
>
> Do not let the headlines make your decisions for you.
>
> [Your Name]

### COMMUNICATION PROMPT 15: Holiday Greeting with Market Tip

**When To Use:** A seasonal holiday greeting that combines a personal touch with a brief, relevant market insight. This keeps you top-of-mind during holiday seasons without being tone-deaf about selling.

**Context To Gather:**
- Holiday or season
- A brief, interesting market observation for the season
- A seasonal home maintenance tip
- A genuine personal note
- Any seasonal real estate opportunity (winter = less competition, spring = more buyers, etc.)

**The Prompt:**

```
You are a real estate agent sending a holiday greeting to your database. This is 80% genuine goodwill, 20% market relevance. It should not feel like a marketing email wearing a Santa hat.

Write a holiday greeting with a market tip. Follow these rules:
- Lead with the holiday greeting -- warm, genuine, not branded
- Transition naturally to one seasonal market observation or home tip
- Include a seasonal maintenance reminder (useful, not salesy)
- End with genuine well-wishes (not a call to action)
- If you mention real estate at all, make it a soft observation, not a pitch
- Length: 100-150 words
- Tone: Warm, human, brief. You are being remembered, not remembered for being annoying.

HOLIDAY DETAILS:
- Holiday/Season: [INSERT]
- Seasonal Market Observation: [INSERT]
- Home Maintenance Tip: [INSERT]
- Personal Note: [INSERT]
- Seasonal Opportunity: [INSERT if applicable]
```

**Example Output:**

> Subject: Happy Thanksgiving -- and one house thing to do this weekend
>
> Hi everyone,
>
> Wishing you a Thanksgiving full of good food, people you actually want to spend hours with, and at most one relative who asks uncomfortable questions.
>
> One quick house thing while you are hosting: run hot water down all your sinks for two minutes sometime this weekend. With all the cooking and cleaning happening, your drains are working harder than usual. A hot water flush prevents the kind of clogs that call a plumber on a holiday weekend.

> Market observation as we head into December: the buyers who are out looking right now are serious. It is a quieter market, but often a more productive one.
